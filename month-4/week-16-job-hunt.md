## Lesson 1: Job Search Strategy

**Sub-topics:**
- Target companies: AI-native startups (Series A-C) as primary targets
- Why Series A-C: they need builders, not researchers; your fullstack + AI combo is gold
- Secondary targets: AI teams at mid-size tech companies
- Why not only FAANG: high bar on DSA, longer timelines, less AI-specific work
- Job boards: LinkedIn, Wellfound (AngelList), Y Combinator Work at a Startup, AI-specific boards
- Company research: what AI stack they use, what problems they solve

**Key Concepts:**

Your strongest fit is AI-native startups at Series A to C stage. These companies are building AI products as their core business, they value builders who can ship end-to-end (not just researchers), and your fullstack background plus GenAI skills is exactly what they need. A company building an AI-powered legal assistant needs someone who can architect the RAG pipeline AND build the frontend AND deploy it -- that is you.

Do not limit yourself to FAANG. While FAANG AI roles are prestigious, the interview bar is extremely high on DSA, the timelines are long (months), and many roles are infrastructure-focused rather than product-focused. Apply to a mix: 60% AI startups, 30% mid-size tech companies with AI teams, 10% large companies if you see a perfect fit. Aim for 3-5 active interview pipelines simultaneously.

## Lesson 2: Warm Outreach

**Sub-topics:**
- Leveraging your EY/TCS network (alumni in tech/AI roles)
- LinkedIn outreach templates (specific, value-offering, non-spammy)
- Engaging with AI community content (meaningful comments, not "Great post!")
- Finding hiring managers vs recruiters (who to contact)
- The informational interview approach (learn about the company, then express interest)
- Following up without being annoying (2 touches max)

**Key Concepts:**

Warm outreach dramatically outperforms cold applications. Your EY and TCS alumni network is larger than you think -- search LinkedIn for former colleagues now at AI companies or tech startups. Your outreach message should be specific: mention something about their company's AI product, share a relevant project of yours, and ask a genuine question. Do not ask for a referral in the first message.

The informational interview is your strongest tool: "I'm transitioning from fullstack to GenAI engineering and building production AI systems. I saw [Company] is working on [specific AI product]. I'd love to hear how your team approaches [specific technical challenge]. Would you have 15 minutes this week?" This positions you as a peer with knowledge, not a job seeker begging for help. After the conversation, if there is a fit, the referral happens naturally.

## Lesson 3: Application Tracking

**Sub-topics:**
- Setting up a tracking system (spreadsheet or tool like Huntr)
- Columns: company, role, stage, contact, follow-up date, notes
- Application velocity: aim for 5-10 quality applications per week
- Quality vs quantity: tailored application > mass spray
- Following up: 1 week after application, 1 week after interview
- Managing multiple pipelines without dropping balls

**Key Concepts:**

A job search is a pipeline management problem. Track every application in a spreadsheet with: company name, role title, date applied, current stage (applied/screen/technical/onsite/offer), key contact, last follow-up date, and notes about the company's AI stack. Update it daily.

Quality beats quantity. A tailored application with a customized cover letter mentioning the company's AI product and linking to your relevant capstone project converts at 5-10x the rate of a generic application. Aim for 5-10 quality applications per week, not 50 generic ones. Always include your portfolio link and a specific capstone that relates to what the company builds.

## Lesson 4: Salary Negotiation Basics

**Sub-topics:**
- AI engineer salary ranges (junior: 80-120K, mid: 120-180K, senior: 160-250K+, varies by geography)
- How to research compensation (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn salary insights)
- The negotiation window: after the offer, before accepting
- Leverage: competing offers, unique skills, market demand
- Beyond base salary: equity, remote work, learning budget, conference budget
- When to negotiate and when to accept

**Key Concepts:**

AI engineering roles command a premium over general fullstack roles because demand outstrips supply. Your combination of fullstack experience + GenAI skills + production portfolio positions you for mid-to-senior AI engineer roles. Research compensation for your target roles and geographies before interviewing so you can state your expectations confidently.

The best leverage is a competing offer. Apply broadly and time your processes so offers arrive close together. When negotiating: express enthusiasm for the role, state your expectation based on research, and frame it as finding a mutually fair number. Beyond base salary, negotiate: equity (especially at startups), remote flexibility, learning budget, and conference attendance. Do not negotiate from a position of desperation -- always have multiple pipelines active.

## Lesson 5: Maintaining Interview Pipelines

**Sub-topics:**
- The 3-5 concurrent pipeline target
- Pacing: align timelines across companies when possible
- Preparing for each stage: screen, technical, system design, behavioral, onsite
- Managing energy and avoiding burnout during the search
- Dealing with rejection (it is a numbers game, not a reflection of your worth)
- When to accept vs when to keep searching

**Key Concepts:**

Maintain 3-5 active interview pipelines at all times until you accept an offer. This gives you leverage (competing offers), insurance (if one falls through), and practice (each interview makes you better). Try to align timelines: if Company A is at the onsite stage, push Company B to move faster so offers arrive within the same window.

Prepare specifically for each company's interview format. Research on Glassdoor and Blind for recent interview experiences. Practice the specific system design scenarios that relate to the company's product. For a legal-tech AI company, practice the legal Q&A system design; for a developer tools company, practice the code review agent design.

## Lesson 6: Continued Learning Post-Roadmap

**Sub-topics:**
- Staying current: follow AI research distillation (not raw papers)
- Key accounts and newsletters: Simon Willison, Latent Space podcast, The Batch
- Building in public: continue the weekly writing habit
- Open-source contributions as learning and visibility
- Depth over breadth: pick one area to go deep (evals, agents, multimodal, infra)
- Community: local meetups, online communities (Discord, Twitter/X)

**Key Concepts:**

The roadmap ends but learning does not. AI moves fast -- the tools and patterns from this roadmap will evolve. Your advantage is not knowing today's tools perfectly; it is having the fundamentals to evaluate and adopt new tools quickly. Follow Simon Willison's blog for practical AI engineering insights, the Latent Space podcast for industry depth, and The Batch (Andrew Ng) for research summaries.

Pick one area to go deep after the roadmap. If you are passionate about evals, build an eval framework and open-source it. If agents excite you, contribute to LangGraph or build a novel agent application. If infrastructure is your strength, build and blog about scaling patterns. Depth in one area makes you memorable in interviews: "I'm the person who built that eval framework" is a stronger identity than "I know a little about everything."
